# The One Principle I Would Never Break

Never build features because they sound impressive.

Build capabilities.

Every capability should answer this question:

> **"Does this move me closer to a world where humans stop operating software and start expressing intent?"**

If the answer is **yes**, build it.

If the answer is **no**, even if customers ask for it, think carefully before adding it.

That's how you avoid becoming "another productivity app" and instead steadily build toward an Intent Operating System.

Yes. In fact, **that's the strategy I would recommend**.

The important shift is this:

> **Don't fund the vision first. Let customers fund the vision.**

Think of it as climbing a staircase where each step:

1. solves a real problem,
2. generates revenue,
3. funds the next step,
4. adds a capability you'll need for the Intent OS.

For example:

| Stage | Product                   | Monthly Price                 | Purpose                                |
| ----- | ------------------------- | ----------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| 1     | AI Meeting Prep           | $49–99                        | Validate demand and earn first revenue |
| 2     | AI Client Memory          | $99–199                       | Build persistent business context      |
| 3     | AI Follow-up & Automation | $199–399                      | Introduce safe execution               |
| 4     | AI Business Operator      | $300–800                      | Coordinate multiple workflows          |
| 5     | Intent Operating System   | Enterprise / platform pricing | Coordinate business goals              |

As revenue grows, you reinvest it into:

* better AI models,
* more integrations,
* infrastructure,
* engineering,
* product design,
* customer support.

This approach has several advantages:

* You validate each assumption before investing heavily.
* Customers tell you what they actually value.
* Revenue reduces dependence on investors.
* Every feature contributes toward your long-term architecture.

One thing I'd be careful about is **not trying to maximize short-term revenue at the expense of the vision**. It's tempting to keep adding unrelated customer requests until you become another CRM or project management tool.

Instead, ask for every feature:

> **"Does this strengthen my ability to understand intent, remember context, plan work, or execute actions?"**

If yes, it's moving you toward the Intent OS.

If no, it may generate some revenue now but could pull you away from your long-term goal.

The strongest companies often look like this in hindsight:

* They started with a narrow, valuable product.
* That product generated cash.
* The cash funded the next capability.
* Over years, those capabilities compounded into a platform.

That's a much more sustainable path than spending years building the entire platform before anyone is willing to pay for it.
